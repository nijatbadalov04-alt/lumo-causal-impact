# Limitations

The study is deliberately honest about what it can and cannot identify.

- **The ODM is operator-agnostic.** It confirms the *market* grew but cannot split Lumo's own share
  from the incumbent's contemporaneous growth (its new fleet and a leisure-led recovery). The
  identified estimand is the effect of corridor entry on the total market, not Lumo's isolated share.

- **Entry is not randomly assigned.** Lumo chose the largest air-competitive corridor, so the design is
  supported by placebo and pre-trend evidence rather than by an instrument; we know of no credible
  instrument for route choice.

- **A pandemic-spanning window.** The treatment straddles the COVID-19 demand collapse and recovery,
  leaving only two clean pre-treatment years.

- **Spillovers (SUTVA) cut both ways and are not bounded.** The incumbent's line-wide competitive
  response (cheaper advance fares, extra capacity) lifts the whole East Coast corridor, so
  within-corridor controls are themselves partly treated and bias those estimates toward zero;
  conversely, off-corridor comparators include West-Coast flows depressed by Avanti's disruption, which
  can inflate the off-corridor gap.

- **Air-abstraction is a corridor-level figure.** Some London–Edinburgh air is Heathrow hub-feed that
  cannot switch to rail, so the ~65% abstraction share is an upper-ish corridor estimate.

- **The ticket-type mechanism is inconclusive.** Fare-class splits begin only in 2021–22, leaving no
  pre-Lumo baseline, so the leisure-composition shift is read from the Stevenage commuter contrast
  rather than from fare-class data.

- **No structural demand model.** Air and rail fares are paywalled, which precludes estimating a
  structural price-elasticity model; the analysis is reduced-form.

- **External validity is narrow.** The setting is air-competitive, premium, long-distance corridors;
  Lumo's case is close to a best case, and the pre-pandemic Grand Central replication is single-station.
